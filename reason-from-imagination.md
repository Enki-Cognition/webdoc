# **Reasoning comes from our imagination.**

## **1\. Introduction**

The historical “blank slate” debate has shifted over the last century. While Enlightenment thinkers such as John Locke argued that humans are born without mental content, modern cognitive science and neuroscience demonstrate that we arrive with significant innate structure—especially in the form of instinctual responses and perceptual biases.

This paper proposes a new synthesis: humans are reasoning blank slates. We are born with instinctual and imaginative systems, but without pre-installed reasoning systems. Crucially, reasoning is not a separate cognitive module—it is a specialized, culturally developed mode of the imagination system. Imagination itself is an evolved extension of our instinctual systems, enabling humans to generate hypothetical scenarios, including the highly structured “world of reason.”

## **2\. Instinctual Systems**

Instinctual systems are pre-programmed, domain-specific action programs shaped by evolutionary pressures. They:  
\- Produce rapid, adaptive responses without propositional thought.  
\- Operate for survival, not truth.  
\- Are programmable in two ways:  
  1\. External programming: Early in life, instinctual responses are shaped by experience via conditioning and social learning.  
  2\. Internal programming: Once reasoning capabilities emerge, instinctual responses can be deliberately modified through reappraisal, mental rehearsal, and habit formation.

These systems are the foundation of human cognition, but by themselves cannot produce knowledge because they do not generate understood justification.

## **3\. Imagination as an Extension of Instinct**

The imagination system evolves directly from the abstraction and scenario-weighting abilities found in animal instincts. In non-human animals, imagination is constrained—used for limited scenario testing. In humans, it is vastly expanded:  
\- Generates counterfactuals, symbolic worlds, and long-term plans.  
\- Remains emotionally and motivationally biased by instinct.  
\- Functions as a flexible, generative workspace for simulating possibilities.

Imagination bridges instinctual impulses and higher-order conceptual frameworks. It makes possible the emergence of reasoning.

## **4\. Reasoning as a Mode of Imagination**

Reasoning is an imagined world within the imagination system, governed by structured justification and inference rules. It:  
\- Emerges through cultural scaffolding—language, education, and norms of argument.  
\- Constrains imagined scenarios according to logic, evidence, and uncertainty modeling.  
\- Produces understood justification, allowing some beliefs to qualify as knowledge.

In this framework, the epistemic distinction is clear:  
\- Imagination without reasoning mode → adaptive action or true belief without justification (non-knowledge state).  
\- Imagination with reasoning mode → justified true belief with understood justification (knowledge).

## **5\. Developmental Pathway**

1\. Instinct-dominant phase (infancy): Behavior governed by instinct; programming is purely external.  
2\. Expanding imagination (childhood): Narratives, play, and counterfactual scenarios.  
3\. Reasoning emergence (later childhood/adolescence): Cultural transmission of reasoning practices; self-programming begins.  
4\. Mature epistemic agency (adulthood): Reasoning is fully integrated with imagination, guiding both knowledge formation and instinct regulation.

## **6\. Stochastic Realist Framing**

Under Stochastic Realism:  
\- All cognitive systems—instinctual, imaginative, reasoning—operate under probabilistic constraints.  
\- Instinct encodes evolutionary priors.  
\- Imagination introduces generative variability.  
\- Reasoning explicitly models uncertainty and justification.

These systems form a feedback loop:  
\- Reasoning → Instinct: deliberate reprogramming.  
\- Instinct → Imagination: directs focus toward emotionally salient content.  
\- Imagination → Reasoning: provides scenarios for evaluation.

## **8\. Conclusion**

Humans are not overall blank slates—we are born with complex instinctual architectures and an expansive imagination system. But we are reasoning blank slates: the structures for explicit justification do not exist at birth and must be constructed through culture and development.

Reasoning is best understood as a specialized imaginative practice embedded within the broader imaginative system, itself rooted in instinct. This model preserves evolutionary continuity, explains the programmability of instinct, and grounds knowledge in understood justification within a stochastic world.
