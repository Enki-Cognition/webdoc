# Processing \- Reasoning vs. Rationalization

We have created a very complex, dynamic, cross domain, digital world. The rules of this world are very different from the old world. To reason about this world we need to change how we reason.

Human cognition often presents itself as a seamless flow of thought, but underneath this flow are very different kinds of processes. Two of the most important are reasoning and rationalization. Both produce beliefs, but their reliability, and predictability diverge sharply.

Reasoning is forward-focused. It starts from evidence, premises, observations or intuition and attempts to reach a justified conclusion. A conclusion that is repeatable and predictable. A justified true belief.  Reasoning includes evaluation of validity, repeatability, and fit to evidence. Its quality depends on how well the process tracks truth (which requires assessing uncertainty and bias). Reasoning requires openness: willingness to revise or abandon conclusions when evidence and logic contradicts them. It often involves discomfort, since it challenges prior commitments. Reasoning is a pathway toward uncertainty-managed justified true belief.

Rationalization on the other hand, is backward-focused. It starts with a preferred conclusion and searches for and creates grounds to support it. Rationalization aims to preserve comfort or coherence: by defending a pre-existing belief, it works to reduce cognitive dissonance. Rationalization is evaluated by plausibility and persuasiveness. Its quality depends on how effectively its conclusions feel acceptable and intuitive, regardless of their actual truth. Rationalization forcefully resists revision: it seeks stability of belief by reinterpreting, minimizing, or selectively ignoring evidence. It provides comfort, even when it distorts. Rationalization is epistemically destructive: it imitates the form of justification but undermines the goal of truth-discovery. While it may still serve psychological or social functions, it does so at the cost of epistemic integrity.

Both reasoning and rationalization start with intuition \- which is the formation of unproven beliefs based on our instincts.

**Intuition** is a subconscious, instinct-driven belief-forming process that produces unjustified impressions or judgments.

**Rationalization** is a belief-forming process that merges subconscious instinct with conscious reasoning to defend existing beliefs, producing falsely justified beliefs..

**Reasoning** is a conscious, justification-driven belief-forming process that integrates intuitive inputs and logically tests them for repeatability and predictability, thereby producing knowledge (justified true beliefs).

# Rationalization Had to Come First

Before imagination, humans would have behaved much like other animals: driven by instinctual learning and environmental conditioning. Our actions followed from patterned responses to stimuli — there was perception, reaction, and adaptation, but no *belief*. Intuition, in that proto-human state, was simply the **output of the instinctual system** — an adaptive signal directing action in a stable and predictable physical world. When early humans evolved imagination — the capacity to model possibilities not immediately present — a new kind of cognitive loop emerged: the ability to *reflect* on instinct. But reflection demanded narrative closure. The mind could not tolerate unstructured intuition; it needed to make sense of it. And so rationalization was born.

Rationalization was not originally a defect. It was the imaginative interpretation of instinct — the story we told ourselves to make sense of why we acted as we did. Because the physical and social environments of early humans were relatively stable, these rationalizations generally aligned with experience. The intuitions they explained were shaped by direct interaction with the same world the narratives described. In that context, coherence and truth were nearly the same thing. The rationalized story worked because the world did not change faster than our intuitions could adapt.

# Reverting to Rationalization Under Stress

Rationalization persists — and reasserts itself under stress — because it integrates imagination, emotion, and instinct into a single, efficient fast-acting system. When we are threatened, the reasoning system, which is slow and effortful, is suppressed in favor of intuitive action. Yet because imagination never fully turns off, the mind automatically begins constructing narratives to justify those instinctual outputs. The story soothes dissonance and restores a sense of control. Thus, under stress, we revert not to pure instinct but to rationalized instinct. Rationalization is the psyche’s way of preventing fragmentation when the reflective system cannot safely operate.

In evolutionary terms, this fallback made perfect sense: during acute threats, coherence preserved group unity and decisive behavior. Truth could wait; survival could not. The trouble is that modern humans live in a constant state of cognitive and social stress, causing the rationalizing mode to dominate far beyond its adaptive window.

# The Modern World Requires Reason Instead of Rationalization

The environment that once rewarded coherent narratives has been replaced by one that demands accurate models. In a world of complex, rapidly changing systems — technological, ecological, informational — instinctual patterns are no longer reliable, and our effective world is more than just  the stable physical world. The complex computer based world is  no longer stable enough for instinctual rationalizations to work. Coherence without truth now misleads; it produces policies, ideologies, and personal decisions that feel right but fail catastrophically when used in reality. The adaptive function of rationalization has become inverted: instead of stabilizing behavior it now destabilizes our behaviors.

Reasoning, by contrast, is the method by which humans can use their intuitive and narrative capacities while testing them against evidence and reproducibility. It does not reject intuition; it repurposes. It does not require certainty, instead it embraces uncertainty explicitly \- focusing on “good enough” instead of perfection. In our engineered, dynamic complex world, reasoning is the only reliable successor. 

# Knowledge Justified True Belief

\[ADD\] 

Since Plato, knowledge has been modeled as Justified True Belief (JTB):

* A proposition must be true.  
* A subject must believe it.  
* The belief must be justified.

This model stood largely unchallenged for two millennia then in 1963 Edmund Gettier asserted that you can have all three conditions and still feel like something is missing. His cases (coins in the pocket, Brown in Barcelona, stopped clock, etc.) use the same trick:

* The subject’s justification seems solid from the inside.  
* The belief turns out true by luck.

The philosopher's intuition 'That’s not real knowledge\!' and concluded: JTB is insufficient.

However what the philosophers missed \- the 'justification' in Gettier cases is only emotional justification \- what feels reasonable, based on instinct, habit, or limited reasoning. It is not epistemic justification in any robust sense. In Smith’s coin case, for example, the reasoning runs through a false lemma. That alone makes the justification epistemically invalid \- it fails any test of error-awareness (uncertainty)  or repeatability.

The appearance of a counterexample only comes from philosophers conflating the feeling of justification with actual justification.

# Annex A: Smart Thinking: Applied Reasoning Worksheet

## Two Ways Your Brain Thinks

When you have a belief or make a choice, your brain figures out *why*. It uses two main ways to do this: **Reasoning** and **Rationalizing**.

| Feature | 1\. Reasoning (The Honest Way) | 2\. Rationalizing (The Excuse Way) |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Starts With** | **Facts and Evidence**  What do I know is real? How do I know it?  | The **Answer You Like**  What makes me feel good? |
| **Goal** | To find the **real truth** and the correct answer even if it goes against our initial feelings. | To find **excuses** and stories to support the answer you already have and protect our feelings |
| **Direction** | **Thinking Forward:** Facts → Answer. | **Thinking Backward:** Answer → Excuses. |
| **Challenge** | You must be ready to change your mind if you find new facts. | You fight against any facts that make your answer look bad. |
| **Feeling** | Sometimes **uncomfortable** because you might have to admit a mistake. Ultimately more comfortable because of valid confidence. | Very **comfortable** because your belief is protected. |

---

## The Thinking Checklist: Are You Reasoning or Rationalizing?

Ask yourself these four questions to check your thinking:

### 1\. Where Did I Start?

* **Reasoning:** Did I start by asking, “What are the facts?”  
* **Rationalizing:** Did I start by thinking, “How can I prove I'm right?”

### 2\. How Do I Handle New Facts?

* **Reasoning:** Am I willing to say, “Oops, I was wrong,” if the facts don't match my conclusion?  
* **Rationalizing:** Do I try to **ignore, blame, or argue** against facts that make my answer look bad?

### 3\. What Am I Searching For?

* **Reasoning:** Is my main goal to find the **most honest, correct answer**?  
* **Rationalizing:** Is my main goal to feel **comfortable and not guilty**?

### 4\. How Does It Feel?

* **Reasoning:** Does this thinking feel a little **difficult** or like **hard work**?  
* **Rationalizing:** Does this thinking feel very **easy** and like I am **defending myself**?

---

**The Takeaway:** To make the best decisions in a complicated world, always try to use **Reasoning** to find the truth, instead of **Rationalizing** to protect your feelings.
